Research

My research work is at the boundary between mathematics and application - for biological modelling in particular. I have been developing model, methods and algorithms for neurodegenerative diseases, oncological applications and multi--modal imaging in general.
Below is a list of problems I have dealt with - the most recent ones first.

Mechanisms of protein propagation in neurodegenerative diseases

Nowadays my major interest is in the application of inverse problem and graph theory techniques for modelling neurodegenerative diseases development and progression. In particular I am interested in the construction of spatio--temporal mechanistic models, whose attempt is to explain disease progression in terms of pathogenic protein propagation through anatomical or functional networks in the brain, which thus induce measurement changes sequentially along the nodes of a network. The pattern of propagation depends on, and can thus reveal, mechanisms of propagation.
Here the long--term neurodegeneration patterns as estimated by my Mechanistic Profiles Model (github repository) from short--term T1-MRI data of Alzheimer's disease data (left) compared with the actual AD progression (right).

Compartmental model for glucose metabolism in cancerous tissues

My PhD studies have been mainly devoted to the study of physiological and pathological behaviour of glucose metabolism in tissue, using micro-PET data. I have been modelling the hepatic and the renal metabolism, under normal or cancerous conditions. I have studied the impact of anticancer drugs by measuring the effects on the parameters estimated by my models.
Here the model for glucose metabolism in the liver and results on mice when treated with metformin, as opposed to control or fasted mice. We observed a significant decrease of the coefficients describing glucose absorption.
